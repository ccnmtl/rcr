Problems with the Federal Regulations The NIH - which is interested in evaluating how institutions were complying with the 1995 regulations - began a series of "not for cause" visits. Site visits did uncover several areas of concern: As the details of policy and enforcement are left to the discretion of the institutions, there is great potential for lax compliance.

  • There is no consensus regarding the nature or the magnitude of a financial interest that raises concerns.
  • Current PHS limits for "significant" financial interests may be unrealistic.
  • The focus of the regulations is exclusively on tangible financial interests; the regulations do not address the more elusive intangible academic conflicts of interest.
  • There is no policy to address institutional or IRB conflicts of interest.
  • There are investigator concerns with an invasion of privacy and breach of confidentiality.
  • Generally, institutions do not have adequate outreach and education for their faculty.

In sum, the NIH found that policies that it thought were clear were open to interpretation. This finding was reinforced by Cho and her colleagues, who found that university conflict-of-interest standards vary widely. It is likely that the USPHS will revise the regulations to address these concerns. (Cho MK, Shohara R, Schissel A, Rennie D (2000). Policies on Faculty Conflicts of Interest at US Universities. JAMA; 284(17); 2203-2208.)
